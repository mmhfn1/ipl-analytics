An analysis of IPL match data, 2008–2025

What actually predicts winning in the IPL

A ball-by-ball statistical analysis of every complete IPL season on record.

This project applies six statistical models to 278,034 regular deliveries from eighteen complete IPL seasons (2008–2025). The raw dataset contains 289,673 rows; 11,468 are from the incomplete 2026 season (excluded), and 171 are super-over balls from 16 tied matches — used to recover the true winner of each tie but not included in analysis. The methods include a win-probability model calibrated on actual match outcomes, a player-role classifier built from standardized (Z-scored) performance statistics, and four regression-based studies covering team composition, player consistency after a trade, replacement value, and roster stability. Every figure below is computed directly from the underlying data — the code that produced it is included, and the Method section documents what was checked, what changed during review, and what the data could not support.

League-wide results, six models

Results across the full dataset

The six results below are computed across all 278,034 deliveries and 1,169 matches, independent of any team or season filter. Each one states what was measured, what was found, and — where relevant — what an earlier, smaller-sample version of this analysis got wrong.

Model 01 — Leverage Index

Pressure Gauge

Each delivery is assigned a Leverage Index: the absolute change in modeled win probability caused by that one ball. A leverage value near zero means the ball changed little about who's likely to win; a value near 1 means it (effectively) decided the match. This section averages that value by phase of the innings — Powerplay (overs 1–6), Middle (7–15), and Death (16–20) — to see which part of an innings tends to matter most.

The Powerplay shows the highest average Leverage Index of the three phases, at 0.0261, compared with 0.0194 for the Death overs and 0.0120 for the Middle overs — about a third higher than the Death overs and more than double the Middle overs. In other words, early wickets and fast starts move the modeled win probability more, on average, than events later in the innings, across the full 18-season dataset. This is an average, not a rule: the single highest-leverage delivery on record came at the end of a chase, not the start — Rahul Tewatia's six off Odean Smith, which sealed Gujarat Titans' win over Punjab Kings in April 2022.

Model 02 — Archetype Classification

Role Scanner

Every player-season (3,137 of them) is assigned one of four roles — Powerplay Aggressor, Anchor, Middle-Over Squeezer, or Death Specialist — based on standardized (Z-scored) performance statistics for that season, not on reputation or a manual label. Batters are scored against the three batting roles, bowlers against the two bowling roles, using whichever metric defines that role (e.g. powerplay strike rate for Powerplay Aggressor, death-overs economy for a bowling Death Specialist).

As of this revision, every player-season receives one of the four roles; there is no "Unclassified" category. An earlier version of this model required at least 60 balls faced or bowled in a season to be classified at all, and grouped everyone below that threshold into "Unclassified" — 28.1% of all player-seasons, league-wide. That conflated two different things: a player whose role genuinely couldn't be determined, and a player who simply didn't get many opportunities that season. The fix keeps the Z-score baseline (the mean and standard deviation each player is compared against) computed only from the stable population — players with at least 60 balls — so a handful of small-sample outliers can't distort it, but every player is then scored against that fixed baseline and assigned a role. Players below the 60-ball threshold are flagged is_low_sample so the distinction is visible rather than hidden. As a check, the model's 2025 classifications were compared against well-known player reputations with no manual adjustment: Kohli (Anchor), Rohit Sharma (Powerplay Aggressor), Bumrah (Middle-Over Squeezer), and Dhoni, Chahal, and Narine (all Death Specialist) — exactly as a cricket fan would expect.

Model 03 — Strategic Philosophy Mapping

Blueprint Index

This model tests whether a team's mix of roles predicts its win percentage. For each of the 156 team-seasons on record, each role's share of squad participation is correlated against that team-season's win percentage. The correlation coefficient (r) ranges from -1 to +1 and measures how closely two variables move together; the p-value estimates the probability of seeing a relationship this strong by chance alone if no real relationship existed — by convention, p < 0.05 is treated as a reasonably reliable signal.

The strongest relationship in this model is between Low-Sample Share (the participation share held by below-threshold players, see Model 02) and win percentage: r = -0.36, p < 0.001. Teams that rely more heavily on fringe, low-sample contributors tend to win less, and this relationship is the most statistically reliable one found across all four archetypes and the Low-Sample Share metric. Among the archetypes themselves, only Anchor share has its own reliable positive relationship with winning (r = +0.245, p = 0.002). Powerplay Aggressor share has a weak negative relationship (r = -0.159, p = 0.047, at the edge of conventional significance). Middle-Over Squeezer and Death Specialist show no statistically reliable relationship in either direction (p = 0.67 and p = 0.32, respectively) — for these two roles, the data does not support a claim that more of that archetype predicts more wins.

Model 04 — Behavioral Analysis

Mindset Monitor

When a player changes teams but keeps the same archetype, does their performance stay roughly where it was ("Stable"), or shift toward the level typical of their new team's players in that role ("Adaptable")? 824 team changes were tracked across the dataset; 378 of those involved a player keeping the same archetype before and after the move, which is the comparable subset reported here.

Anchor is the least stable role after a team change: only 54.2% of same-role Anchor moves are classified Stable, compared with 66-71% for the other three archetypes. Of the Anchors classified Adaptable, about 63% shifted toward their new team's typical Anchor performance level, which is consistent with "anchoring" being shaped substantially by what a given lineup needs rather than being a fixed trait of the player. Death Specialist is the most stable role (69.8% Stable), suggesting finishing ability is comparatively more portable across team changes.

Model 05 — Liability & Replacement Value

Value Finder

814 player-seasons fall in the bottom 25% of their archetype's performance distribution, league-wide. For each one, this model estimates "Replacement Delta": the projected change in team win percentage if that player-season were swapped for a league-median performer in the same role. The estimate is built from a regression of team win percentage on that archetype's average quality across all team-seasons — see the Method section for how a negative or unreliable regression result is handled.

Of the four archetypes, only Powerplay Aggressor has a regression slope that is statistically reliable (p = 0.043) when computed across the full 18-season dataset. Anchor, Death Specialist, and Middle-Over Squeezer all produce slopes that are not distinguishable from zero once pooled across this much data (p = 0.91, 0.997, and 0.93, respectively) — in plain terms, this analysis cannot reliably say that improving a team's average quality in those three roles changes its win percentage, even though the underperformers themselves are identified correctly. Rather than report a number derived mostly from statistical noise, Replacement Delta is floored at 0 percentage points for those three roles: an underperformer in those roles is still flagged, but is not assigned a win% estimate the data can't support.

Model 06 — Tenure & Ranking Impact

Momentum Meter

League standings are reconstructed match by match, in chronological order, using only the data available at the time of each match (a team's rank entering a match never uses that match's own result). This identifies stretches where a team held a Top-5 league position for two or more consecutive matches — three examples are plotted alongside: Mumbai Indians' 2020 title run, Gujarat Titans' debut-season title in 2022, and Royal Challengers Bengaluru's 2025 title.

192 Top-5 streaks of two or more matches were identified across the 18-season dataset. This module also tests whether a more experienced, longer-tenured roster is associated with a more stable strategic identity from one season to the next. An earlier, six-season version of this analysis reported a fairly strong relationship (r = +0.54) between roster tenure and strategic consistency. Recomputed across the complete 18-season dataset, that relationship is no longer present: r = +0.003, p = 0.97 (n = 139), which is statistically indistinguishable from no relationship at all. The underlying data was checked for errors and none were found, so this is reported as a genuine result rather than corrected to match the earlier finding: eighteen years cover several distinct tactical eras of T20 cricket (rule changes, the rise of higher-scoring batting approaches, and the 2023 Impact Player rule among them), and a single correlation pooled across all of them does not capture a relationship that may have held within any one era.

Corrections and limitations

Method and revision notes

This analysis went through a data-quality review after an initial version was published, prompted by a question about whether the season range used was actually complete. The points below document what was found and fixed. Several of them changed real numbers by a meaningful margin, and are recorded here rather than silently corrected.

01

Win-probability terminal states now defer to the actual match winner. The most common chase result in T20 — a team falling short without being bowled out (e.g. 142/9 chasing 158) — was previously left to the regression instead of being forced to a clean loss, because the required-run-rate formula collapses to 0 once no balls remain, making a lost cause look "on pace." This was producing a win probability around 85-95% on the final ball of 127 of 373 matches in an earlier, smaller version of this dataset. Final-ball win probability now matches the real outcome in all 635 decided matches checked.

02

Rain/DLS detection now uses the correct threshold. The "was this innings interrupted" check used to require fewer than 19 overs bowled; a match shortened to exactly 19 overs fell through the gap. The correct test is simply "didn't complete all 120 balls, wasn't all out, didn't reach the target" — there's no other legitimate reason that combination occurs.

03

Tied matches now resolve to their real Super Over winner. 16 of 1,169 matches have a raw winner value of literally "tie" or "no result", not a team name. Ties are now resolved by reading the Super Over innings (comparing runs scored, including the rare case of a second Super Over when the first one also tied) before that data gets filtered out of the main ball-by-ball analysis. Genuine no-results are excluded from win% and training data, and correctly awarded 1 point each in the standings reconstruction rather than 0-0.

04

Season labels are now derived from match dates, not parsed from the label string. Three season labels in the source data use a "yyyy/yy" format, and there's no single rule that resolves all of them correctly — "2007/08" is IPL Season 1, played in 2008, while "2020/21" is the pandemic-displaced 2020 season, played entirely in 2020. Taking "the first year" gets one right and one wrong. Every label's true calendar year is now looked up directly from its matches' actual dates, which is unambiguous for all 19 raw labels in the file.

05

A no-ball is now correctly counted as a ball faced. It wasn't being credited toward a batter's strike rate, even though a no-ball (and its free hit) is very much faced and often hit for runs. Wides remain correctly excluded.

06

Archetype classification no longer has an "Unclassified" bucket. The Z-score baseline for each metric is computed only from the stable, ≥60-ball population so a handful of extreme small-sample outliers can't distort it — but every player-season is then scored against that fixed baseline and given a real role, flagged is_low_sample rather than dropped.

07

Replacement Delta can no longer go negative for a flagged underperformer. When an archetype's league-wide win-impact slope comes back negative or statistically unreliable, the formula now floors it at 0 for this calculation rather than implying that upgrading a worse player would hurt the team — a previously real, mechanical bug.

08

A previously reported correlation does not hold at full scale. The roster-tenure-density vs strategic-consistency correlation (Model 06) reported r = +0.54 on a six-season window. Recomputed across the complete 18-season dataset, it falls to r = +0.003 (p = 0.97). The underlying data was checked for missing values, duplicate rows, and outliers; none were found, so this is reported as the correct result for the full dataset rather than reverted to the earlier figure.
